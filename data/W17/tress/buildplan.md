# Build Plan: Tress 2026

## Overview

Tress 2026 is a stylist-first booking and discovery platform for Black women seeking natural hair services. It's built around verified stylist profiles with live calendars, shoppable product links, and community-driven discovery—turning the original insight (finding the right stylist and products) into a direct revenue model through booking commissions rather than ads.

The market shift is simple: creator platforms now monetize through transactions, not scale. TikTok proved short-form video discovery works; Shopify proved embedded commerce works; and the natural hair market has matured into a $9B+ category with established demand. Tress can win by being the only platform that combines all three—discovery feed, verified stylist booking, and product commerce—in one place built specifically for this community.

Go-to-market is stylist-led, not consumer-led. Recruit 50 anchor stylists per city (Atlanta, Houston, DC) before launch, give them free verified profiles and first-mover advantage, and let them bring their clients. ## Why Now?

## Current Market Analysis

**Market Size:** The global Black hair care market was cited at approximately $9 billion at the time of Tress's operation (Newsweek, December 2016). A precise 2026 figure is not available in the research report. However, the directional signal is clear: major CPG brands including Unilever (SheaMoisture parent company), P&G (Pantene Gold Series), and L'Oréal (Mizani, Dark & Lovely) have all expanded dedicated multicultural hair portfolios since 2018, indicating the commercial opportunity has grown, not contracted.

## Competition Map:

The competitive landscape has shifted meaningfully since 2016, but no direct successor to Tress's core thesis — a structured, community-first platform purpose-built for Black women's hair — appears to have emerged.

- **Instagram/TikTok:** Still the dominant venues for hair content, but remain general-purpose platforms with no structured stylist-product-price metadata, no native booking, and no community Q&A. The original Tress diagnosis remains accurate.
- **Pinterest:** Strong in inspiration and visual search, weak in community, booking, and Black hair specificity. Its demographic skew toward Western users limits relevance for African markets.
- **Booksy/StyleSeat:** Solve the booking problem but offer no content discovery, community, or inspiration layer. Users must already know what stylist they want.
- **NaturallyCurly.com:** Established editorial community for textured hair, but lacks social mechanics, booking, and a mobile-first product experience.
- **Mindbody:** Enterprise-focused, not community-driven; no cultural specificity.

**The gap:** No current platform combines algorithmic hair content discovery, structured stylist metadata, native booking, and shoppable product links in a single mobile experience built specifically for Black women. That gap is the rebuild opportunity.

**Demand signals from adjacent products:** LTK's 40M+ users demonstrate that niche creator commerce works at scale. Booksy's growth in urban US markets confirms Black women are already booking hair appointments digitally. TikTok's #BlackHairTok content category (exact view counts unavailable) demonstrates sustained organic demand for this content format.

## Recommended MVP

## Core Features:

## Algorithmic Inspiration Feed with Structured Metadata

A TikTok-style scrolling feed of short video and photo posts, each tagged at upload with stylist name, location, products used, and estimated price range. Unlike the original Tress feed — which relied on follow-based network effects that required years to build — this feed surfaces content algorithmically based on hair type, style preference, and location, generating value for new users from their first session. This directly addresses the original's distribution failure by removing the cold-start problem for individual users.

## Native Stylist Booking with Commission Revenue

Each stylist profile includes a live availability calendar and one-tap booking, with Tress collecting a 10–15% commission per appointment. This is the monetization model the original team articulated ("we want Tress to be the platform you think of when you want to connect with a stylist") but never built. Unlike Booksy or StyleSeat, booking here is embedded inside a discovery and community context — users find a style they love, see who created it, and book that specific stylist in three taps.

## Shoppable Product Links on Every Post

Creators and stylists tag the specific products used in each post, linked to purchasable inventory via Shopify's Storefront API or LTK affiliate integration. Tress earns affiliate revenue on each product sale. This replaces the ad-scale-dependent model entirely — a post with 200 views that drives three product purchases generates real revenue. The original Tress envisioned product discovery as a feature; the rebuild makes it a revenue line from day one.

## Verified Stylist Profiles with Portfolio and Pricing

Stylists claim verified profiles with portfolio galleries, service menus, price ranges, and client reviews. This is the structured data layer the original Tress built for user posts, now formalized as a professional tool. It gives stylists a reason to actively maintain a presence on the platform — solving the supply-side cold-start problem — and gives consumers the actionable information the original app promised.

## What We Will NOT Build (MVP):

- Computer vision / visual hair search (technically available via Google Cloud Vision API, but adds complexity without proving the core booking + commerce loop first)
- Live video / streaming features
- A web platform (mobile-first only)
- A general beauty or wellness expansion beyond Black hair
- A marketplace for physical product inventory (affiliate links only; no fulfillment)

## Success Metrics with Concrete Thresholds:

- 500 verified stylist profiles within 90 days of launch
- 1,000 completed bookings within 6 months (validates commission revenue model)
- 40% of registered users return within 7 days of first session (retention benchmark for community viability)
- $10,000 gross merchandise value in affiliate product sales within 6 months
- 25% of new user installs attributable to organic referral or social share (validates distribution improvement over original)

## Go-to-Market Strategy

## Target Customer Segment:

Black women aged 22–38 in three US cities with high Black population density and established natural hair communities: Atlanta, Houston, and Washington D.C. This is deliberately narrower than Tress's original global launch. The original's 90-country user base was a vanity metric that masked the inability to build deep engagement in any single market. A city-by-city rollout builds the local stylist supply and community density that makes booking and discovery genuinely useful before expanding.

## Primary Distribution Channel + Tactics:

The primary channel is stylist-led organic growth, not consumer marketing. Recruit 50 anchor stylists per city before consumer launch, offering them free verified profiles, zero commission for the first six months, and co-branded content support. Each stylist's existing Instagram and TikTok following becomes a distribution channel — when a stylist posts "book me on Tress," their audience downloads the app. This inverts the original Tress model, which tried to grow consumers first and attract stylists later. Supply-side seeding is the proven playbook from Booksy and StyleSeat's early growth.

Secondary channel: TikTok creator partnerships with mid-tier Black hair influencers (100K–500K followers) in target cities. Exact influencer rates unavailable, but mid-tier partnerships are generally accessible at $500–$2,000 per post — within a modest seed budget.

## Pricing Strategy:

- **Consumers:** Free. No subscription required to browse, book, or engage.
- **Stylists:** Free verified profile for the first 6 months; 12% booking commission thereafter (below Booksy's reported 20–30% range, creating a direct competitive incentive to switch).
- **Brand partnerships:** Direct CPG sponsorship packages for brands targeting the platform's audience, available once the platform reaches 10,000 monthly active users. This is the revenue stream that was unavailable to the original Tress at its scale but is now accessible given documented CPG investment in multicultural hair.
- **Affiliate commerce:** 5–8% affiliate margin on product sales via LTK and Shopify integrations, earned from day one.

## Differentiation vs. 2026 Competitors:

Booksy has booking but no community or discovery. Instagram has community but no booking, no structured metadata, and no cultural specificity. A rebuilt Tress is the only platform where a Black woman can watch a stylist's video, identify the exact products used, book that stylist, and ask the community a follow-up question — all without leaving the app. That integration is the moat. The original Tress had the community vision but not the revenue infrastructure. The rebuild has both.
