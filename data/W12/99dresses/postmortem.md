## Founding Story

Nikki Durkin was 18 years old and already on her third business when she conceived 99dresses in 2010 as a post-HSC (Higher School Certificate) project in Sydney, Australia.<sup><a href="https://www.smartcompany.com.au/startupsmart/advice/startupsmart-growth/y-combinator-picks-99dresses-founder-nikki-durkin/">[1]</a></sup> The idea was simple and personal: women accumulate clothes they stop wearing, yet feel guilty buying more. A platform that let them trade unwanted items for virtual currency—spendable only on other women's unwanted items—could close that loop and make shopping feel guilt-free.

Before writing a single line of code, Durkin validated the concept through a Facebook event. She sent it to 250 friends and received 20,000 RSVPs within a month, eventually reaching 40,000.<sup><a href="https://itwire.com/profiler/durkins-dreams-and-dresses.html">[2]</a></sup> That signal was enough to attract Pollenizer, a Sydney-based startup incubator co-founded by Mick Liubinskas, who mentored Durkin through the initial build. The site went live in September 2010.<sup><a href="https://itwire.com/profiler/durkins-dreams-and-dresses.html">[2]</a></sup> Around December 2010, Pollenizer and 99dresses parted ways under terms that are not publicly documented.<sup><a href="https://www.crunchbase.com/organization/99dresses">[3]</a></sup>

The Australian pilot ran through 2011 and demonstrated real demand: 4,500 dresses were uploaded and 3,500 were sold.<sup><a href="https://techcrunch.com/2012/03/27/yc-demo-day-winter-2012-session-5/">[4]</a></sup> Women continued emailing Durkin asking when the site would return after it went offline during the US pivot preparation. "I've proven the model in Oz," she told TechCrunch.<sup><a href="https://techcrunch.com/2012/03/26/99dresses-wants-to-give-women-an-infinite-closet/">[5]</a></sup>

In November 2011, Durkin met Peter Delahunty at Fishburners, a Sydney co-working space. Delahunty joined as technical co-founder with one week to build a demo for the Y Combinator application.<sup><a href="https://www.smartcompany.com.au/startupsmart/advice/startupsmart-growth/y-combinator-picks-99dresses-founder-nikki-durkin/">[1]</a></sup> The pair were accepted into YC Winter 2012. A third team member, Dan Walker, is referenced in later reporting but his role and background are not publicly documented.

<media-image src="https://techcrunch.com/wp-content/uploads/2012/03/99dresses.png" alt="TechCrunch article header image for 99dresses 'Infinite Closet' coverage, March 2012" caption="TechCrunch's March 26, 2012 article '99dresses Wants To Give Women An Infinite Closet' — published the day before YC Demo Day — featured Nikki Durkin and the platform's dress-swapping interface."></media-image>

The founding story carries an important structural warning that went unaddressed: at the time of YC Demo Day, the entire company was two people. Durkin was the sole non-technical founder. Delahunty held all the engineering knowledge. That concentration of technical dependency in a single person would prove catastrophic within months.

## What They Built

<media-image src="https://thenextweb.com/wp-content/uploads/2012/01/99dresses.jpg" alt="99dresses website homepage showing the Infinite Closet concept" caption="The Next Web's coverage of 99dresses in January 2012, showing the 'infinite wardrobe' concept where users upload clothes priced in 'buttons', the platform's virtual currency."></media-image>

99dresses was a peer-to-peer fashion marketplace built around a closed-loop virtual economy. The core insight was behavioral: women accumulate clothes they no longer wear but feel guilty spending real money on new ones. The platform resolved that tension by removing cash from the equation entirely—at least for the buyer.

**The Original "Buttons" System (2010–2013)**

The mechanics worked as follows. A seller photographed a dress, listed it on the platform, and priced it in "buttons"—the platform's proprietary virtual currency.<sup><a href="https://techcrunch.com/2012/03/26/99dresses-wants-to-give-women-an-infinite-closet/">[13]</a></sup> Buttons could be purchased for $1 each and could only be spent within the 99dresses ecosystem. When a buyer purchased an item, the seller received buttons, which she could then spend on someone else's listing. The cycle was designed to keep value circulating within the community rather than leaking out to cash withdrawals.

This mechanic served two strategic purposes. First, it created a psychological reframe: spending buttons felt less like spending money, reducing purchase friction. Second, it built a captive economy—users who accumulated buttons had an incentive to stay on the platform to spend them. Durkin's pitch line at YC Demo Day captured the emotional logic precisely: "I have created crack for women."<sup><a href="https://venturebeat.com/2012/03/27/99dresses-crack-for-women/">[14]</a></sup>

The user experience in the Australian version was straightforward: photograph your unwanted clothes, upload them, set a button price, and wait for a buyer. When a sale completed, the seller received buttons and shipped the item. The buyer received a piece of clothing she had effectively "traded" rather than purchased. The platform's revenue came from selling buttons to users who wanted to buy without first selling.

<media-image src="https://venturebeat.com/wp-content/uploads/2012/03/99dresses.jpg" alt="99dresses at YC W12 Demo Day, March 27 2012" caption="VentureBeat's coverage of 99dresses at YC W12 Demo Day on March 27, 2012, where co-founder Nikki Durkin famously declared 'I have created crack for women.'"></media-image>

**The iOS Pivot and "Karma" System (September 2013)**

American users did not respond to the buttons system the way Australian users had. The cultural gap was real: US users wanted mobile-first, instant-gratification commerce. "We love the concept. We want to swap stuff, but if it's not on mobile, like, forget it," users told Durkin.<sup><a href="https://mixergy.com/interviews/nikki-durkin-99dresses-interview/">[9]</a></sup>

In September 2013—roughly 18 months after Demo Day—99dresses launched an iOS app and went mobile-only. The redesigned product introduced a "karma" system for fast fashion: items retailing under $60 could be traded peer-to-peer using karma points rather than cash, preserving the guilt-free mechanic for lower-priced items. Higher-end items could be bought and sold using real money.<sup><a href="https://angel.co/company/99dresses">[15]</a></sup> The hybrid model attempted to serve both the trade-oriented community and users who wanted straightforward resale.

<media-image src="https://techcrunch.com/wp-content/uploads/2013/09/99dresses-ios.jpg" alt="99dresses iOS app screenshots showing the karma-based trading interface, September 2013" caption="TechCrunch's September 30, 2013 coverage of the 99dresses iOS app launch, showing the redesigned mobile interface with the new 'karma' system that replaced the original buttons-only economy for US users."></media-image>

The iOS app represented a fundamental rethink of the product, not an iteration. The original closed-loop economy was partially dismantled. The karma system was a new mechanic that had not been validated at scale. And the company was building this pivot with a depleted team, compressed runway, and in a market that had been filling with competitors for 18 months.

What made 99dresses different from alternatives like eBay or Poshmark was the virtual currency mechanic—the idea that you could refresh your wardrobe without net cash outflow. That differentiation was real in Australia. In the US, it arrived too late and in a market where Poshmark, ThredUp, and others had already established cash-based resale as the default behavior.

## Market Position

### Target Customers

99dresses targeted women aged roughly 18–35 who regularly bought fashion items, accumulated unworn clothing, and felt the psychological friction of spending money on new clothes while existing clothes went unused. The platform's emotional hook—guilt-free shopping, the thrill of a new item without the cost—was aimed specifically at fashion-conscious consumers who shopped frequently enough to generate a steady supply of tradeable inventory. The Australian pilot confirmed this segment existed and was willing to engage: 4,500 items uploaded and 3,500 sold in a limited pilot demonstrated both supply and demand.<sup><a href="https://techcrunch.com/2012/03/27/yc-demo-day-winter-2012-session-5/">[4]</a></sup>

The US expansion targeted the same demographic but in a market with different behavioral defaults. American consumers in this segment were already being served by cash-based resale platforms. The virtual currency mechanic, which had been a differentiator in Australia, required behavioral change from US users who had already formed habits elsewhere.

### Market Size

The US secondhand apparel market was large and growing during 99dresses' operational window. The broader peer-to-peer fashion resale category attracted significant venture capital between 2012 and 2014, with multiple well-funded competitors entering the space. The market's size was not the constraint for 99dresses—the company's problem was not that the market was too small, but that it arrived too late with a product that required users to adopt an unfamiliar currency mechanic in a category where cash-based alternatives were already established.

### Competition

The competitive landscape that Durkin described as having "flooded the space" by the time the US product launched included several well-capitalized platforms. Poshmark, founded in 2011, built a cash-based peer-to-peer fashion marketplace and raised a Series B in 2013. ThredUp, founded in 2009, operated a managed resale model. Tradesy launched in 2012. Each of these platforms used real money, which required no behavioral change from users accustomed to cash transactions.

99dresses' virtual currency model was its core differentiator and its core liability. In Australia, where the platform launched without established competitors, users adopted the buttons mechanic because it was the only option. In the US, users had already formed habits with cash-based alternatives. Asking them to learn a new currency system—and to trust that their buttons would retain value—was a higher adoption barrier than the company could clear in the time its runway allowed.

A telling data point from Durkin's post-mortem illustrates the competitive gap: a comparable startup in a different vertical had 3x fewer trades, 2.5x fewer members, and twice the team size in its first 8 months of US operation—yet that startup went on to raise a Series A while 99dresses failed.<sup><a href="https://mozyrko.pl/wp-content/uploads/2014/10/77-failed-startup-post-mortems.pdf">[16]</a></sup> The implication is that execution quality, team stability, and fundraising narrative mattered as much as raw metrics in this market environment.

## Business Model

99dresses generated revenue through two primary mechanisms. The first was direct button sales: users who wanted to buy items without first selling their own clothes purchased buttons at $1 each. This created immediate cash inflow and subsidized the closed-loop economy. The second was transaction fees on exchanges within the platform, though the specific fee structure is not publicly documented.

The model's structural weakness was that it worked best when users were actively trading—uploading items, earning buttons, and spending them. As transaction prices decreased (driven by the fast-fashion nature of the inventory), revenue per transaction declined. The planned expansion into higher-value categories like handbags and accessories, which would have supported higher button prices and larger transaction fees, failed to gain traction in the US market.<sup><a href="https://www.failory.com/cemetery/99dresses">[17]</a></sup>

The iOS pivot introduced a hybrid model: karma points for items under $60, cash transactions for higher-end items. This attempted to address the revenue-per-transaction problem but added complexity to a product that was already struggling to find US product-market fit.

## Traction

## Post-Mortem

Durkin's June 2014 Medium post-mortem is one of the most candid founder failure accounts in the YC canon. She catalogued the full arc: "Since then I've survived being stabbed in the back by cofounders, investment rounds falling through, massive technology fuckups that brought sales to a halt, visa problems, lack of money, lack of traction, lack of a team, hiring the wrong people, firing people I didn't want to fire, lack of product-market fit, and everything else in between."<sup><a href="https://nikkidurkin.medium.com/my-startup-failed-and-this-is-what-it-feels-like-c5d64b3ae96b">[8]</a></sup> What follows is an analysis of those failures, organized by causal weight.

<media-hn url="https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=7933155" title="My startup failed, and this is what it feels like" points="" comments=""></media-hn>

### 1. Co-Founder Departure Triggered a Funding Collapse That Permanently Compressed Runway

The single most damaging event in the company's history occurred in 2012, shortly after YC Demo Day. Durkin had signed a $1.2 million seed round with a group of investors. The day after the paperwork was signed, both co-founders—Peter Delahunty and Dan Walker—left the company without warning. The $1.2 million never reached the company's account.<sup><a href="https://nikkidurkin.medium.com/my-startup-failed-and-this-is-what-it-feels-like-c5d64b3ae96b">[8]</a></sup>

The mechanism of damage was twofold. First, the funding collapse cut the company's capital base in half. Durkin managed to close a reduced round of $595,000—roughly half the intended capital—with at least one investor telling her: "Shit happens. Take the money and go sort it out."<sup><a href="https://nikkidurkin.medium.com/my-startup-failed-and-this-is-what-it-feels-like-c5d64b3ae96b">[8]</a></sup> Crunchbase lists Draper Associates and Pegasus Tech Ventures among the investors, though the full participant list for the $595K round is not confirmed.<sup><a href="https://www.crunchbase.com/organization/99dresses/financial_details">[18]</a></sup>

Second, and more consequentially, the departure of the technical co-founder left Durkin without engineering leadership at the precise moment the company needed to build a US product. The months spent recruiting a replacement—Marcin Popielarz eventually joined as technology manager—were months in which competitors were scaling.<sup><a href="https://techcrunch.com/2013/09/30/with-launch-of-ios-app-99dresses-goes-mobile-only-for-dress-swapping-goodness/">[10]</a></sup> The attempted remedy—closing a reduced round and rebuilding the team—was the right response, but it could not recover the lost time or capital. The company entered the US market with half the intended resources and a new technical team that needed time to ramp.

### 2. The Australia-to-US Translation Problem Was Underestimated

The "buttons" virtual currency mechanic was the product's core differentiator. In Australia, it worked: users adopted the closed-loop economy, traded actively, and generated the 78% sell-through rate that made the Australian pilot compelling. Durkin arrived at YC Demo Day with a credible claim: "I've proven the model in Oz."<sup><a href="https://techcrunch.com/2012/03/26/99dresses-wants-to-give-women-an-infinite-closet/">[5]</a></sup>

What the Australian data could not reveal was whether the mechanic's success was product-driven or context-driven. In Australia, 99dresses launched into a market with no established peer-to-peer fashion resale competitors. Users adopted buttons because it was the only option. In the US, Poshmark, ThredUp, and Tradesy had already established cash-based resale as the default behavior. Asking US users to learn a new currency system—and to trust that their buttons would retain value—was a materially higher adoption barrier.

The team's attempted remedy was the September 2013 iOS pivot: replacing buttons with a "karma" system for fast fashion and introducing cash transactions for higher-end items.<sup><a href="https://angel.co/company/99dresses">[15]</a></sup> This was a fundamental product rethink, not an iteration. It required rebuilding the core mechanic from scratch, 18 months after Demo Day, with a depleted team and compressed runway. By Durkin's own assessment: "The market had moved on, competitors had flooded the space and the product we had built just didn't provide enough value in comparison."<sup><a href="https://nikkidurkin.medium.com/my-startup-failed-and-this-is-what-it-feels-like-c5d64b3ae96b">[8]</a></sup>

### 3. Non-Technical Founder Dependency Created Structural Fragility

Durkin was not a developer and, by her own account, did not know how to manage developers. "I thought by then I'm not well-versed with technology, I didn't have any idea on how to code, and I truly did not know how to work with developers as well. So I was not able to understand some of the issues we were facing," she said in a Mixergy interview.<sup><a href="https://mixergy.com/interviews/nikki-durkin-99dresses-interview/">[9]</a></sup>

This dependency had two consequences. First, when Delahunty left, Durkin had no ability to assess the state of the codebase, manage the transition, or evaluate replacement candidates from a position of technical knowledge. Second, the "massive technology fuckups that brought sales to a halt" she references in her post-mortem—the specific nature of which is not publicly documented—were problems she could not diagnose or resolve independently.<sup><a href="https://nikkidurkin.medium.com/my-startup-failed-and-this-is-what-it-feels-like-c5d64b3ae96b">[8]</a></sup>

The attempted remedy was hiring Marcin Popielarz as technology manager and eventually bringing on additional engineers. This stabilized the technical team but did not resolve the underlying dynamic: Durkin remained dependent on technical co-founders whose departure had already nearly ended the company once. The structural fragility of a two-person founding team—with all technical knowledge concentrated in one person—was a risk that went unaddressed until it became a crisis.

### 4. Visa Complications Burned Runway at a Critical Moment

Durkin applied for and eventually received an O-1 visa (a US visa for individuals with extraordinary ability), but was subjected to additional processing at the US consulate in Sydney. The delay kept her out of the US for approximately one month while the startup burned cash.<sup><a href="https://mixergy.com/interviews/nikki-durkin-99dresses-interview/">[9]</a></sup> For a company operating on $595,000 in total capital—already half of what was planned—a month of burn without the CEO present to manage the team was a meaningful setback.

The visa dependency also created a structural vulnerability at shutdown: when 99dresses closed in June 2014, Durkin had 10 days to sell her possessions and leave the US, as her O-1 visa was tied to her CEO role.<sup><a href="https://nikkidurkin.medium.com/my-startup-failed-and-this-is-what-it-feels-like-c5d64b3ae96b">[8]</a></sup> The visa issue was not a primary cause of failure, but it compounded every other problem by adding time pressure and geographic constraints at the worst possible moments.

### 5. The 18-Month Execution Gap Was Fatal in a Fast-Moving Category

The cumulative effect of the co-founder departure, funding collapse, team rebuild, visa delays, and product pivot was an 18-month gap between peak external validation (YC Demo Day, March 2012) and a functional US mobile product (iOS launch, September 2013). In a consumer marketplace category where network effects compound quickly, 18 months is a long time to be absent.

The comparative data point from Durkin's post-mortem makes this concrete: a startup in a comparable vertical had 3x fewer trades and 2.5x fewer members in its first 8 months of US operation, yet went on to raise a Series A.<sup><a href="https://mozyrko.pl/wp-content/uploads/2014/10/77-failed-startup-post-mortems.pdf">[16]</a></sup> 99dresses had better metrics but worse execution and a weaker fundraising narrative—the result of 18 months of visible instability that made it difficult to raise additional capital to extend runway.

Durkin accepted full responsibility for the outcome: "I take complete responsibility for this failure."<sup><a href="https://nikkidurkin.medium.com/my-startup-failed-and-this-is-what-it-feels-like-c5d64b3ae96b">[8]</a></sup>

## Key Lessons

- **A two-person founding team with all technical knowledge in one person is a single point of failure, not a team.** 99dresses had no redundancy in its engineering capability. When Delahunty left, the company lost its entire technical foundation overnight. Founders building in technical domains without coding ability need either to learn enough to manage developers effectively, or to build a team with enough depth that no single departure is existential. Durkin's direct response—founding CodeMakers to teach programming—reflects how clearly she understood this lesson.<sup><a href="https://medium.com/adoptastartup/fail-99dresses-7b493c339879">[12]</a></sup>

- **Product-market fit in one geography does not transfer automatically, especially when the core mechanic requires behavioral change.** The buttons system worked in Australia because it was the only option. In the US, it competed against established cash-based alternatives. Before expanding geographically, founders should test whether their differentiation is product-driven (it would work anywhere) or context-driven (it works because there's no competition). The 18-month delay between Demo Day and the US iOS launch gave competitors time to establish cash-based resale as the default, making behavioral change even harder to achieve.<sup><a href="https://techcrunch.com/2013/09/30/with-launch-of-ios-app-99dresses-goes-mobile-only-for-dress-swapping-goodness/">[10]</a></sup>

- **Fundraising narrative and execution stability matter as much as raw metrics in early-stage consumer marketplaces.** Durkin's post-mortem data point—a comparable startup with worse metrics that raised a Series A—suggests that investors were pricing team stability and execution quality into their decisions. The visible instability of 99dresses (co-founder departures, reduced round, team rebuilds) made subsequent fundraising harder, compressing runway precisely when the company needed more time to find US product-market fit.<sup><a href="https://mozyrko.pl/wp-content/uploads/2014/10/77-failed-startup-post-mortems.pdf">[16]</a></sup>

- **Visa and immigration constraints are operational risks that compound every other problem for international founders.** Durkin's O-1 visa delay cost approximately one month of runway and kept her physically absent from her team during a critical period. International founders building US companies should treat visa processing as a critical path item, not an administrative detail, and should plan for delays that will occur at the worst possible moments.<sup><a href="https://nikkidurkin.medium.com/my-startup-failed-and-this-is-what-it-feels-like-c5d64b3ae96b">[8]</a></sup>

- **The startup press covers launches and raises, not the years of perseverance that precede failure.** Durkin noted that "failing is lonely and isolating" and that the startup community rarely shares raw accounts of companies that persevered for years but ultimately failed.<sup><a href="https://nikkidurkin.medium.com/my-startup-failed-and-this-is-what-it-feels-like-c5d64b3ae96b">[8]</a></sup> Her post-mortem became a cultural artifact precisely because it filled that gap—and its viral reception on Hacker News confirmed that founders recognized the experience she described.<sup><a href="https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=7933155">[11]</a></sup>
