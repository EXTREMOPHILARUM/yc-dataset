# Build Plan: Grouper 2026

## Overview

Grouper 2026 is a AI-powered group dating platform that arranges curated outings for small groups of friends—not strangers. The product targets women 24–32 in major U.S. cities who are fatigued by algorithmic dating apps and looking for lower-pressure social discovery. Instead of swiping profiles, users opt into monthly group dinners or bar outings where the matching happens via LLM analysis of interests, values, and social compatibility—no human curation required.

The viability shift: large language models now solve Grouper's original bottleneck. In 2016, matching required expensive human judgment. Today, Claude or GPT-4 can ingest user preferences and generate compatible groups at under $2 per match, making unit economics sustainable at a $25–35 price point with 40%+ repeat rates.

The go-to-market angle is precision targeting: launch in one city, acquire through dating app fatigue messaging and word-of-mouth within tight friend networks, and prove retention before scaling. ## Why Now?

## Current Market Analysis

**Market size:** The U.S. online dating market was approximately $1B annually when Grouper operated (2012–2016). By 2024, it had grown to approximately $3.5B (Statista, 2024), with Match Group alone reporting $3.4B in 2023 revenue. However, the more relevant market framing for a rebuilt Grouper is the emerging "social facilitation" category — structured IRL experiences for adults — which does not yet have a clean market size estimate. Timeleft's rapid expansion to 100+ cities without disclosed funding rounds suggests the category is real but not yet institutionally sized.

## Competition map and gaps:

- **Timeleft** (2022–present): Closest direct analogue — strangers at a dinner table, weekly cadence, ~€27/dinner in European markets. Key weakness: no friend-group format (solo attendance only), no romantic framing, European-first with limited U.S. density, and no AI personalization layer beyond basic questionnaires.
- **Bumble BFF**: Massive user base but purely digital — no facilitated IRL experience, no venue coordination, no group format. Weakness: the product stops at the match; users must self-organize the actual meeting.
- **Hinge** (Match Group): Dominant in the 25–35 dating demographic but explicitly one-on-one, browse-and-message. No group format, no safety-by-design architecture. Weakness: women's dissatisfaction with the format is well-documented and growing.
- **Meetup.com**: Group-based but interest-driven, not socially curated, and skews older. No romantic framing, no AI matching, no venue coordination.
- **Fever/Eventbrite**: Ticketed experiences, not curated social matching.

**The gap:** No current competitor combines the friend-group format (structural safety), AI-driven compatibility matching, automated venue booking, and a clear romantic-adjacent positioning. That combination is unoccupied.

**Demand signals from adjacent products:** Bumble BFF's existence as a standalone feature within a dating app confirms that users want facilitated IRL connection but find the dating-app format insufficient. Timeleft's growth confirms willingness to pay for curated stranger dinners. The "third place" discourse (widespread in 2023–2025 media) signals cultural readiness.

## Recommended MVP

## Core features:

**1. AI Group Matching Engine.** Using Claude 3.5 (June 2024) or GPT-4o (May 2024) via API, the system ingests user-provided interest data, optional Spotify taste signals, and a short onboarding questionnaire to generate 3+3 group matches without human reviewers. This directly replaces Grouper's fatal cost center. Unlike the original, matching runs at software marginal cost — the quality floor is maintained by model capability, not headcount. Users invite one or two friends, or the system assembles a group from solo sign-ups (a key expansion of the original's coordination requirement).

**2. Automated Venue Booking via OpenTable/Resy API.** The system selects and books a reserved table at a vetted partner venue based on neighborhood, availability, and group size — no manual bar-partnership infrastructure required. This eliminates the 400-partner-bar operational burden Grouper carried. Unlike the original, venue quality is enforced programmatically through rating filters, not relationship management.

**3. Pre-Event Blind Briefing + Post-Event AI Follow-Up.** Participants receive a curated "what to expect" briefing with conversation starters generated by the AI — no names, no photos, preserving the information blackout that was Grouper's core safety and excitement mechanic. Post-event, an automated follow-up (replacing Grouper's human CRM team) asks for feedback and surfaces opt-in contact exchange. Unlike the original, this scales to any volume without staffing.

**What we will NOT build:** In-app messaging, profile browsing, a swipe interface, or solo-user matching without at least one friend. These features would recreate the dating-app category Grouper's founders correctly identified as broken, and would invite direct competition with Hinge and Bumble on their strongest ground.

## Success metrics:

- 40%+ of users who complete one outing book a second within 60 days (retention threshold)
- Cost per match below $2 (AI + API costs combined)
- Net Promoter Score ≥ 50 after first outing
- 500 completed outings in launch city within 90 days of launch

## Go-to-Market Strategy

**Target customer segment:** Women aged 24–32 in a single dense U.S. city (New York or Chicago recommended for launch) who have used a dating app in the past 12 months, report dissatisfaction with it, and have at least one friend willing to co-sign up. This is the narrowest viable segment — it mirrors Grouper's original core user, but targets the dissatisfaction signal explicitly rather than the general "young urban professional" framing. Women are the acquisition priority because their participation determines the product's safety perception and drives male sign-up organically, exactly as Bumble's women-first model demonstrated.

**Primary distribution channel:** Organic seeding in female-skewing online communities — specifically Reddit (r/datingadvice, r/femalefashionadvice, r/AskWomen), TikTok creators in the "dating app fatigue" niche, and direct outreach to Bumble BFF users via content marketing. No paid acquisition in the first 90 days. The goal is to generate the kind of "I tried this weird thing and it was actually great" word-of-mouth that Grouper earned organically in 2012–2013, now amplified by social media infrastructure that didn't exist then.

**Pricing strategy:** $25/person per outing (slight increase from Grouper's $20, justified by inflation and the AI-quality positioning), with a $49/month membership tier that includes two outings and priority matching. The membership tier directly addresses Waxman's acknowledged failure to communicate membership value clearly — it is the primary revenue model, not the transactional fee. Subscription billing via Stripe eliminates the payment friction Grouper experienced.

**Differentiation vs. 2026 competitors:** Against Timeleft — friend-group format and romantic-adjacent positioning. Against Bumble BFF — the experience doesn't end at the match; the venue is booked, the table is reserved, the conversation starters are ready. Against Hinge — no browsing, no messaging, no profile optimization anxiety. The pitch is one sentence: *"We do everything except show up."*
