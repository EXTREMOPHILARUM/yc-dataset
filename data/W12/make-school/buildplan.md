# Build Plan: Make School 2026

## Overview

By 2026, Make School is a fully remote, two-year applied CS program for working adults transitioning into tech—priced through a compliant, capped income share agreement with employer co-investment options. It targets 22–32 year-olds already earning $35k–$60k who need credentials without leaving their jobs or taking on debt risk.

The regulatory environment has shifted decisively. The CFPB's 2021 ISA classification and subsequent bootcamp settlements have created a playbook for compliant income-based financing—one that actually works better than the original model because transparency and caps build trust instead of inviting litigation. Meanwhile, AI tutoring has matured enough to deliver personalized instruction at scale, eliminating the unit economics problem that plagued the first version.

The rebuild launches under an existing regional accreditor's partnership (not chasing independent accreditation), with transparent employer placement data audited quarterly and published publicly. This isn't positioning against four-year degrees—it's positioning alongside them, for people who can't afford to pause their careers. ## Why Now?

The single most important change since Make School's 2021 collapse is regulatory clarity on income share agreements. In 2021, the CFPB formally classified ISAs as credit products subject to Truth in Lending Act disclosures, and multiple states have since passed ISA-specific consumer protection legislation. This eliminates the legal ambiguity that allowed Make School's ISA structure—engineered with Vemo Education—to generate obligations the Student Borrower Protection Center documented at up to $250,000 per student. A rebuilt program can now deploy a compliant, capped ISA modeled on Purdue University's Back a Boiler program, which caps total repayment at 2.5x the funded amount, making the liability ceiling transparent and legally defensible before a single student enrolls.

Three additional structural shifts compound this regulatory opening. First, LLM-based instruction tools—specifically GPT-4 (March 2023) and Claude 3 (March 2024)—enable adaptive, project-based CS curriculum delivery at near-zero marginal cost per student. Make School's unit economics required ISA amounts large enough to cover expensive human instruction at scale; that constraint is now substantially reduced. Second, GitHub Copilot (launched October 2021) and Replit's AI-assisted IDE (2023) have compressed the time required to produce working software projects, accelerating project-based learning outcomes that were Make School's core pedagogical differentiator. Third, the remote-first hiring market validated by COVID-19 eliminates the need for a San Francisco campus—the physical infrastructure that consumed a significant portion of Make School's $30M raise (exact allocation not publicly disclosed).

On distribution: employer-sponsored education pipelines have matured into proven channels. Amazon Career Choice, Google Career Certificates, and Salesforce Trailhead now represent documented employer co-investment models. A rebuilt program can structure direct employer partnerships that reduce or eliminate student financial obligation, removing predatory lending risk at the structural level rather than patching it contractually.

The U.S. coding bootcamp market reached approximately $1.4 billion in 2023 (Coursera Industry Report 2024; exact figure should be independently verified), with demand for credentialed, job-linked CS education continuing to outpace supply from traditional universities.

## Current Market Analysis

The alternative CS education market has grown substantially since Make School operated. U.S. coding bootcamp enrollment was approximately 23,000 students annually in 2019 (the last pre-COVID benchmark from industry surveys); post-pandemic figures are not independently verified in the research provided, but adjacent signals—including a 25% year-over-year increase in online CS enrollment reported by NCES through 2022—suggest continued expansion. The specific TAM for accredited two-year applied CS degrees remains unquantified in public data; this is a gap a rebuild team should validate before Series A.

## Current competitor map and weaknesses:

- **BloomTech (formerly Lambda School):** The most direct ISA-model successor. Faced its own regulatory action in 2023 (California DFPI settlement) and has retreated from the accredited-degree positioning entirely, now operating as a non-accredited bootcamp. Weakness: no degree credential, damaged brand trust, ongoing regulatory scrutiny.
- **Springboard and Coursera:** Offer certificate-based online programs with mentor support. Weakness: no accredited degree, limited employer placement infrastructure, no ISA financing.
- **Western Governors University:** Accredited, competency-based, low-cost. Weakness: self-paced model produces low completion rates (exact figures not publicly disclosed), no project-based cohort structure, no employer co-investment pipeline.
- **Community colleges with CS programs:** Low cost, accredited. Weakness: four-year timelines, limited industry curriculum alignment, no income-contingent financing.
- **Outlier.org and Nexford University:** Online degree programs with lower price points. Weakness: limited employer brand recognition, no structured job placement.

**Demand signals from adjacent products:** GitHub Copilot's 1.8 million paid subscribers (GitHub Octoverse 2023) and Replit's 20 million registered users signal massive latent demand for structured, project-based CS skill development that self-directed tools alone cannot satisfy. Employer tuition reimbursement programs remain systematically underutilized—Amazon Career Choice reports that fewer than 10% of eligible employees access benefits annually—indicating a distribution channel with significant uncaptured capacity.

## Recommended MVP

### Core Feature 1: Compliant, Capped ISA with Employer Co-Investment Option

A financing structure modeled on Purdue's Back a Boiler program, capping total repayment at 2.5x the funded amount with full TILA disclosures, deployed only in states with explicit ISA consumer protection frameworks. Students can alternatively access employer co-investment agreements—structured with 5–10 anchor employer partners before launch—that reduce or eliminate personal financial obligation. This directly inverts Make School's fatal flaw: the financing instrument is legally vetted and liability-capped before enrollment opens, not after a regulatory order arrives.

## Core Feature 2: AI-Augmented Project-Based Curriculum

A two-year applied CS curriculum delivered entirely online, using GPT-4-class LLMs (March 2023 capability baseline) for adaptive instruction, code review, and personalized pacing. Each semester culminates in a deployable software project reviewed by employer partners. This preserves Make School's strongest pedagogical differentiator—project-based learning with industry-aligned outcomes—while eliminating the instructional cost structure that made large ISAs necessary. Unlike Make School's static curriculum, the AI layer enables continuous updating without full faculty rebuilds.

## Core Feature 3: Accreditation-First Partnership Structure

Launch under an existing regional accreditor's incubation pathway—using the WSCUC precedent Make School itself established in 2018—but with independent accreditation as a contractual milestone within 36 months, not an aspirational goal. The partnership agreement must include explicit degree-granting continuity provisions protecting enrolled students if the partnership dissolves. This eliminates the borrowed-legitimacy dependency that made Make School institutionally fragile.

## Core Feature 4: Employer Placement Pipeline with Transparent Outcomes Reporting

Structured internship and hiring agreements with 10+ employer partners at launch, with third-party-audited placement and salary data published quarterly. Make School's alleged misrepresentation of $100,000 starting salaries (per SBPC) destroyed trust; radical transparency on outcomes is the differentiation.

**What we will NOT build:** A physical campus. Living expense financing of any kind. Any ISA structure in states without explicit consumer protection frameworks. A proprietary accreditation body.

**Success metrics:** 70%+ two-year completion rate (vs. Make School's documented ~1.5% as of April 2019); 80%+ job placement within 6 months of graduation; zero regulatory actions in first 36 months; ISA default rate below 15%.

## Go-to-Market Strategy

**Target customer segment:** Adults aged 22–32 with some college credit but no four-year degree, currently employed in non-technical roles earning $35,000–$55,000 annually, seeking a credentialed career transition into software engineering. This segment is large enough to validate the model (approximately 36 million Americans have some college but no degree, per NCES 2023), specific enough to build curriculum and employer partnerships around, and financially motivated enough to complete a two-year program. It deliberately excludes recent high school graduates—Make School's original demographic—whose lower financial sophistication increases predatory lending risk and regulatory exposure.

**Primary distribution channel:** Employer partnership pipeline. Anchor the first cohort through 5–10 mid-size tech and tech-adjacent companies (target: companies with 500–5,000 employees that use Amazon Career Choice or equivalent tuition benefit programs) who co-sponsor employee enrollment. This channel bypasses consumer marketing costs, pre-qualifies students with employment history, and creates built-in placement pathways. Secondary channel: LinkedIn Talent Solutions advertising targeting the specific demographic profile above, with cost-per-enrollment benchmarks established in the first 90 days.

**Pricing strategy:** $18,000–$22,000 total program cost (exact figure to be validated against competitor pricing and employer co-investment terms). Employer co-investment covers 50–100% for sponsored students; capped ISA (2.5x maximum, TILA-compliant) available for non-sponsored students in qualifying states. This price point is below BloomTech's historical ISA exposure and well below Make School's documented $250,000 ceiling, while remaining above community college pricing to sustain unit economics without venture subsidy dependency.

**Differentiation vs. 2026 competitors:** BloomTech has retreated from degree credentials; this program offers an accredited B.S. WGU offers accreditation without employer pipeline or cohort structure. Springboard offers neither accreditation nor employer co-investment. The specific combination—accredited degree + AI-augmented curriculum + capped compliant ISA + employer co-investment + audited outcomes data—has no direct 2026 competitor, replicating Make School's genuine market differentiation while eliminating the regulatory and financial structure that destroyed it.
