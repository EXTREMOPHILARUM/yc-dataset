## ❌ The Problem

**75% of people die from diseases we still can't cure (WHO).**

Clinical trials are how we bring new treatments to the world, but today it takes over a decade and more than $1 billion to bring a new drug to market. Nowhere is the problem more obvious than in **patient recruitment.**

Recruitment is the biggest bottleneck:

* **Research sites** read 1000s of medical records and run 30+ min screening calls to enroll just 1 in 10 patients, using phones, spreadsheets, and disconnected systems.
* **Patients:** Wait days or weeks to hear back, face slow and frustrating screening processes, and often drop out due to poor communication and lost trust.
* **Pharma companies:** Lose up to $312M per delayed trial (12.2-month average delay), with 80% of studies missing recruitment targets despite spending 30% of their budgets on it.

## 👷 The Solution

Meet **Polly** - the AI recruiter for clinical trials.

Polly is an AI agent that helps research sites find, interview, and schedule patients for clinical trials. She integrates with the tools the team already uses, so there’s nothing new to learn.

Polly handles recruitment end-to-end, with potentially eligible patients automatically landing in research site calendars for their first visit.

**How Polly helps:**

* **Speed:** Saves team's hours per week by automating medical records reviews and turning 30+ minute screening conversations into 1-minute decisions.
* **Scale & Flexibility:** Engages every patient the moment they express interest, 24/7, and scales on-demand, handling thousands of conversations at once without burdening research teams.
* **Cost:** Automates the most labor-intensive parts of patient recruitment, filling trials faster at a fraction of the cost.

## 🚀 The Vision

Polly is just the start. We’re building the digital workforce for clinical research that will run all back-office trial operations so research teams can focus on patients, not paperwork.

For anyone living with or caring for someone with a chronic condition, one thing is clear: **Developing life-saving drugs shouldn't take a decade.**
