### **🛠️ THE PROBLEM WITH BUILDING INTERNAL TOOLS**

* **Operational teams rely heavily on internal tools.**
* They know best the challenges they need these tools to solve, and have **the most urgency** for them.
* But **they are not autonomous to build and maintain them**.

Why? Because building internal tools requires knowing how to write and maintain code.

_A short story about a simple use case is worth a thousand words…_

John from the Ops team wants to drive his sales team’s productivity by building and sending forms on Slack (with buttons, input, datetime etc.) and process that information back to his CRM, HubSpot. For example, sending a form like this to update deals’ close dates that are past due:

![](https://lh7-us.googleusercontent.com/-4DOLazJmJHGcHsg7aHXz2Ssy1rlRWy3zkaHokyGVxRQ8K05Cz56ohVxpjpkX_qd4RFynIJUOEBIW2dgkIKaPG31AugDRGZEQENIL7nNp-LoeqXd6fUiF3JsEotM1Kdt1jpHjWGL-IoIGI2xFerDYkw)

He looks at options to build this but none of them are suitable:

* **Ask his dev team**: his request is low priority in the backlog and he knows he’ll have to go back often to the dev team for maintenance after the tool is built.
* **Leverage internal tools builders**: these tools are built for developers and require knowing how to code. The technical bar is too high for John to get started and build quickly.
* **Use no-code workflow tools**: his use case requires building end-user interfaces (Slack notifications with a form and buttons), which is something these tools don’t handle easily without having to code.
